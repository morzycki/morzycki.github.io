 Nepalese regard new king with suspicion

Tension on streets as people demand to know truth behind royal bloodbath

Rory McCarthy
Guardian

Tuesday June 5, 2001

As the curfew began in Kathmandu last night, Nepal's deeply spiritual people remained shaken to the core by the murder of their monarch, a man they regarded as an incarnation of god. Many refused to accept the apparently obvious truth that King Birendra and his family were murdered by his lovesick son in a violent row over his marriage plans.

Birendra, who ruled for 29 years until his death on Friday, had become a popular hero despite his brutal repression of democracy rallies in 1990.

At that time, after weeks of street protests against the regime's absolutism, he agreed to instigate a constitutional monarchy and give more power to politicians. His sudden death struck a raw nerve.

"I don't see any hope. The past king was such a great democrat, such a great personality," said Diamon Shumshere Rana, a popular Nepali novelist.

"No one can replace King Birendra, no one can be like him. When I think of my country, everything is dark before my eyes. There is no personality to replace the past king. He was like [the Hindu god] Rama," he said.

The death of a king who was regarded as a god is not easily explained to a poor and largely illiterate people caught up in their own entrenched values. Few gave any credence to the official explanation that the deaths were an unfortunate "accident".

Tantric gods

Kathmandu Valley alone has more than 2,700 shrines, an indication of the depth of religious feeling in the country, where per capita income is less than the price of one night's stay in Kathmandu's top hotels. The illiteracy rate is as high as 70% in the largely rural population.

The Nepalese follow a complex blend of the Hindu and Buddhist religions. The Buddha himself was born in Nepal and Buddhism was later introduced by the Indian emperor Ashoka. Then Hinduism crossed into the Himalayan kingdom, and later a tantric form of Buddhism descended from Tibet. Now a pantheon of tantric gods sit alongside a vast array of Hindu and Buddhist deities.

Vishnu, a four-armed god often pictured holding a conch shell, a disc, a stick and a lotus flower, is regarded as the preserver and as having played a key role in the creation of the universe. Vishnu has 10 incarnations, which follow an evolutionary pattern from the fish to the last incarnation, yet to appear, of Kalki the destroyer. The Nepalese see their own king as another Vishnu incarnation.

"The king is regarded as a god and worshipped," said Vasant Gadre, a professor at Jawaharlal Nehru University in New Delhi. "But is it really so different from England at the time of Shakespeare, when the king was thought to be divine?"

Prof Gadre said there was little doubt that now the Nepali people would have a problem with their concept of their monarch as god.

"In the immediate future there will probably be some kind of intense disil lusionment over a period of time," he said. "They will look back into their own beliefs. Generally we tend to believe that whatever happens is a result of our past actions. I think the people will also reach this conclusion and realise that life goes on."

Birendra presided over the dramatic opening up of the kingdom which, from 1816 to 1951, was closed to the outside world. Now foreign aid has poured in and tourism has taken hold, bringing floods of westerners flocking to Kathmandu in search of the hippy hideaway once centred on the now rundown and largely overlooked Freak Street.

"Just as we are seeing in India, there has been a decline in spiritualism," Prof Gadre said. "Spiritualism is only in books now. We live it personally, but when we go out of our homes we tend to forget it all."

Much of the concern for the future of the monarchy rests not with the new king, Gyanendra, a respected wildlife enthusiast, but with his heir. His eldest son, Paras, is poorly regarded in Nepal because of reports of his wild lifestyle. Many of the rumours spreading through the capital made him responsible in some way for Friday's murders.

Shortly after Gyanendra's procession entered the palace crowds began chanting "Hang the murderer Gyanendra," and within minutes the crowd began throwing stones. Some in the crowd said the new king had conspired with his son to organise the shooting. Gyanendra was out of Kathmandu at the time of the killing, visiting one of Nepal's national parks in Pokhara, but Paras was at the table with his wife Komal. He was unhurt, though his wife suffered a small injury to her finger.

One Indian newspaper reported that thousands of posters were being printed blaming the new king and his son for the murders. Nepalese television networks reported that Crown Prince Dipendra had himself been shot in the back, casting doubt on the original theory that he had shot his family in a fit of rage because they opposed his choice of bride.

Family targeted

Another Indian newspaper pointed out that only the royal family at the table were hit, although the room was crowded with servants and there were security guards nearby.

It is thought Gyanendra was reluctant to blame Dipendra for the murders at the weekend while he was still alive and officially the king. Gyanendra promised at his coronation to hold an investigation and give a true explanation of the deaths.

Yesterday's coronation was the second time Gyanendra had been proclaimed king. He was installed on the throne briefly in 1950-51 as a child after the rest of his family fled to India during the widespread political upheaval that led the opening up of the kingdom.

But the new monarch still refused yesterday to give a credible explanation for the murders, and until he does the Nepalese will be reluctant to hail him as the latest incarnation of god.

Not so long to reign over us... Dipendra was king for just three days. Here are seven other short-lived rulers:

Xerxes II, king of Persia in 424BC, reigned for only 45 days before being murdered by his half-brother, Sogdianus.

Edward V was king of England from April 9 to June 26 1483 when he and his brother were imprisoned in the Tower of London by their uncle, who ascended the throne as Richard III. The boys were killed.

Lady Jane Grey was queen of England for nine days in 1553 at the age of 16, after the death of Edward VI, but was forced to abdicate in favour of Edward's sister, Mary. She was imprisoned in the Tower and later beheaded.

Uthumphorn became the king of Ayatthaya (Thailand) in 1758 but reigned for only 10 days before abdicating in favour of his elder brother, Prince Anurak Montri, who went on to reign as King Ekathat.

Christian Frederick, the crown prince of Denmark, was elected king of Norway on May 17 1814, but was ousted when Norway was taken by Sweden in October of the same year.

Frederick III, emperor of Germany and king of Prussia, ascended the throne in March 1888, but died of throat cancer 99 days later and was succeeded by his son.

King Umberto II of Italy reigned for 27 days in 1946, until the Italian people voted in a referendum to abolish the monarchy and become a republic.
